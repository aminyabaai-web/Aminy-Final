# HealthSource Partners: PE buyer analysis reveals a high-risk, high-reward rural staffing platform

**HealthSource Partners presents a compelling but deeply risk-layered opportunity in a sector where PE's recent track record has been catastrophic.** The company's $2.8M EBITDA (2024) growing to a projected $7.1M (2026) on 100% contracted revenue positions it as a potential rural healthcare staffing platform at a time when three major PE-backed national competitors have failed or entered severe financial distress within 18 months. However, critical customer concentration (two health systems generating effectively all revenue), extreme key person dependency, heavy 1099 contractor reliance (90% of workforce), and single-state operations in North Dakota create a risk stack that demands substantial valuation discounts and structural deal protections. Notably, due diligence should immediately clarify whether the "Trinity Health" client is the national Livonia, Michigan system (AA-/Aa3 rated, 101 hospitals) or the independent, unaffiliated Trinity Health in Minot, North Dakota—a distinction with enormous implications for customer quality and concentration risk.

---

## The PE graveyard in physician staffing sets the stage

The physician staffing and practice management sector has experienced an unprecedented wave of destruction since 2022, reshaping the competitive landscape in ways that both threaten and benefit a company like HealthSource Partners.

**Envision Healthcare**, acquired by KKR for **$9.9 billion** in 2018, filed Chapter 11 in May 2023 with $7.7 billion in debt. KKR lost its entire equity stake. The company emerged in November 2023, split into two weakened entities (Envision Physician Services and AMSURG), with $5.6 billion in debt eliminated. **American Physician Partners**, backed by Brown Brothers Harriman, collapsed even more abruptly—ceasing all operations in July 2023, failing to pay over 1,000 emergency physicians for their final months of work, and filing Chapter 7 liquidation in September 2023. **TeamHealth**, owned by Blackstone since its $6.1 billion LBO in 2016, remains alive but in severe distress with Moody's Ca and S&P CCC- ratings, financial leverage above **20x EBITDA**, and negative cash flow through 2024. **Sound Physicians**, acquired by Summit Partners and OptumHealth for $2.2 billion in 2018, carries an unsustainable **23x leverage** ratio and completed an emergency debt restructuring in May 2024.

The root causes are consistent across all failures: excessive acquisition-driven leverage, dependence on out-of-network surprise billing revenue that the No Surprises Act (effective January 2022) eliminated, UnitedHealthcare network disputes, COVID-era volume declines, and post-pandemic labor cost inflation. Emergency medicine operating margins nationally run approximately **5.7%** excluding OON revenue—a fundamentally thin-margin business that cannot support high leverage.

This carnage creates a genuine window of opportunity for HealthSource. When APP collapsed, its ~150 ED contracts were redistributed: one-third went to smaller physician-owned groups, demonstrating hospitals' willingness to choose regional alternatives. Hospitals increasingly view PE-backed staffing firms' financial instability as an operational risk—a bankrupt staffing partner can leave an emergency department unstaffed. A small, debt-free, relationship-driven regional company with contracted revenue represents the opposite of what has failed.

## Comparable transactions suggest a 5–8x EBITDA valuation range

Recent M&A data and advisory firm benchmarks point to a valuation framework that depends heavily on whether HealthSource is positioned as an add-on acquisition or a standalone platform investment.

For healthcare staffing companies in the **$1–10M EBITDA range**, general market multiples cluster at **4–7x EBITDA**. Physician practice management companies with stronger profiles achieve **5–8x EBITDA** as add-ons and **7–10x** as platforms. The platform-versus-add-on differential is significant: PE firms routinely acquire add-ons at 4–7x EBITDA and build platforms valued at 10–14x at exit, capturing **3–5 turns of multiple arbitrage**.

Several factors specific to HealthSource affect where in this range it would trade. **Positive modifiers** include 100% contracted/recurring revenue (commanding a **1–3x premium** over transactional staffing), a strong EBITDA growth trajectory (~2.5x growth from 2024 to 2026), in-network positioning compliant with the No Surprises Act, and the scarcity/essential-service value of rural healthcare staffing. **Negative modifiers** include extreme customer concentration, sole-owner key person risk, geographic concentration in a single state, government payer mix exposure (rural hospitals skew heavily Medicare/Medicaid), and the post-Envision sector overhang that has compressed hospital-based physician staffing multiples.

**On 2024 EBITDA ($2.8M)**, a realistic range is **4–6x ($11.2M–$16.8M enterprise value)** as an add-on, reflecting the small absolute size and concentration risks. **On 2025 forward EBITDA ($4.0M)**, a platform investment might achieve **6–8x ($24M–$32M)**, rising toward **7–9x on 2026 projected EBITDA ($7.1M) = $49.7M–$63.9M** if the buyer underwrites the growth plan with confidence. The customer concentration alone likely warrants a **20–40% discount** from comparable-company multiples, partially offset by the contracted revenue premium and rural scarcity value.

## CommonSpirit is divesting North Dakota hospitals; Trinity requires clarification

The financial health and strategic direction of HealthSource's two anchor clients present materially different—and in some respects alarming—risk profiles that demand immediate due diligence attention.

**CommonSpirit Health** (137 hospitals, 24 states, ~$40.1B FY2025 revenue) has shown meaningful financial recovery, with EBITDA improving from $748M in FY2023 to **$1.9B in FY2025** and bond ratings upgraded to **A3/A-/A-** with stable outlooks. However, CommonSpirit is **actively divesting its North Dakota rural hospital portfolio**. CHI St. Alexius Health Devils Lake is being sold to Altru Health System with closing on March 1, 2026. CHI St. Alexius Health Bismarck, Turtle Lake, and Garrison are under a Letter of Intent signed January 22, 2026 for potential transfer to Altru. At the January 2026 JP Morgan Healthcare Conference, CommonSpirit's CEO indicated additional divestitures would be announced in the coming quarter. CommonSpirit has also engaged consultants for a broad operational review (Project ImpACT) explicitly targeting contract labor costs as a reduction lever.

**This is a critical finding**: if HealthSource serves these specific North Dakota facilities, its contracts could transfer to Altru Health System—a much smaller (~$700M revenue) regional system with potentially different outsourcing preferences and weaker financial capacity.

**Trinity Health** requires immediate clarification. The national Trinity Health system based in **Livonia, Michigan** (101 hospitals, AA-/Aa3 rated, $25.4B revenue, $15.6B unrestricted cash) does **not operate in North Dakota**. There is a separate, **independent, unaffiliated** Trinity Health based in **Minot, North Dakota**—a small Lutheran nonprofit system with approximately 3 hospitals. The CIM's reference to "Trinity Health (~250 hospitals)" implies the national Livonia system, but if the actual client is the Minot system, the customer quality and total addressable market claims would be dramatically different. The national Trinity Health system is aggressively insourcing clinical staffing through its **FirstChoice** internal staffing agency, having cut contract labor costs **25.5% in FY2024** with further reductions continuing. This insourcing trend presents a direct competitive threat.

The broader industry trend supports caution: per Kaufman Hall's 2025 survey, only **45% of health systems outsource clinical staff**, down from 53% in 2023. Systems are bringing clinical staffing in-house while outsourcing non-clinical functions. Community Health Systems has insourced 500+ hospitalist and EM physicians, saving **$4M per quarter** versus prior outsourced arrangements.

## Industry dynamics favor small operators but structural headwinds persist

The U.S. healthcare staffing market totals approximately **$19.5–21.6 billion** (2024–2025), with physician staffing specifically at **~$6.05 billion** growing at a 4.3% CAGR. The locum tenens segment—most relevant to rural staffing—has been the fastest-growing at **15–17% annually** in 2023–2024 and is projected to reach $14.6 billion globally by 2030.

The No Surprises Act has permanently restructured the economics of emergency medicine staffing. The law banned balance billing for emergency services, capping patient cost-sharing at in-network rates and forcing remaining disputes into Independent Dispute Resolution (IDR). PE-backed companies dominate IDR: the top 4 filing entities (TeamHealth, SCP Health, Radiology Partners, Envision) account for **60–70% of all disputes**, and PE-backed groups win **90% of their cases** versus only 39% for non-PE groups. For a small company like HealthSource, the practical implication is that the PRG revenue model—where the company collects and keeps professional fees—is now constrained by NSA payment benchmarks, and building IDR capacity requires investment in billing sophistication.

Rural hospital financial fragility is a persistent concern. **46% of rural hospitals** have negative operating margins, and **432 rural hospitals** are vulnerable to closure per the Chartis Group's February 2025 analysis. Since 2010, 182 rural hospitals have closed or converted. Proposed Medicaid spending reductions of up to **$880 billion** over the next decade would disproportionately devastate rural hospital clients, 69% of whose closures occur in states that have not expanded Medicaid. However, the Rural Emergency Hospital (REH) designation program (42 REHs operating as of October 2025) creates a new category of rural facility that mandates 24/7 ED coverage—a natural market for outsourced staffing companies.

The emergency medicine physician market presents a paradoxical dynamic: ACEP projects a **surplus of ~7,845 emergency physicians by 2030**, driven by declining residency competitiveness and reduced demand. EM residency applications dropped **16.8%** in 2021–22 and another **18.1%** in 2022–23. This potential surplus could ease HealthSource's physician recruitment challenges in rural areas but may also reduce pricing power for staffing companies over time.

## Small regional operators hold genuine competitive advantages

HealthSource's positioning as a small, relationship-driven, rural-focused staffing company is actually a **competitive strength** in the current environment, not a weakness. The competitive advantages of regional players include faster decision-making ("speedboat vs. cruise ship"), deeper physician and hospital administrator relationships, better physician retention (physicians feel valued rather than treated as corporate inputs), lower overhead with no PE profit extraction or debt service, and perceived stability after the collapse of three national competitors.

After APP's collapse, hospitals redistributed contracts broadly: TeamHealth received ~20 EDs, SCP Health ~16, USACS 18, and Vituity 11, but dozens of smaller groups received 1–3 contracts each. Rural hospitals especially cannot afford staffing disruption and value continuity above scale. The **SCP Health model** provides an instructive comparison: with moderate leverage (**5x EBITDA** versus TeamHealth's 20x+), greater in-network revenue share, and a favorable cost structure, SCP Health has been "more resilient than some of its larger competitors" per Moody's. This validates that the winning formula in physician staffing post-2022 is moderate leverage, in-network positioning, and operational efficiency—precisely the profile HealthSource can offer a PE buyer.

Physician-owned competitors like **Vituity** (3,500+ physicians, zero debt, 90%+ physician retention on contract transitions) and **USACS** (299 EDs, 95%+ physician-owned) set the standard for stability. For HealthSource, the key competitive moat is the combination of hospital switching costs (credentialing, workflow integration, quality metrics continuity) and the difficulty of recruiting physicians to rural markets. Once a staffing company is embedded in a rural hospital, the barriers to switching are substantial.

The PE track record specifically in physician staffing has been almost uniformly negative. Envision (KKR), TeamHealth (Blackstone), APP (Brown Brothers Harriman), Sound Physicians (Summit/Optum), and US Anesthesia Partners (Welsh Carson, now facing an FTC antitrust lawsuit) all demonstrate the same pattern: excessive leverage, aggressive revenue strategies, and insufficient respect for the thin-margin, relationship-driven nature of the business. The successful models maintain **moderate leverage** (≤5x), preserve **physician autonomy**, and prioritize **in-network revenue**. Any PE buyer of HealthSource should internalize these lessons.

## Six critical risk factors demand rigorous due diligence

A PE buyer evaluating HealthSource faces a concentrated risk stack that requires both structural deal protections and post-close operational investment.

**Customer concentration is the most critical risk.** With effectively 100% of revenue from two health systems across four hospitals, HealthSource far exceeds standard PE thresholds (single customer >10% of revenue is a red flag; top 5 >25% is concerning). The loss of even one hospital contract would eliminate approximately 25% of revenue. This concentration typically warrants a **20–40% valuation discount**. Mitigation requires long-term contracts with 12–24 month termination notice periods, aggressive client diversification post-close, and expansion of service lines within existing clients to deepen switching costs. The CommonSpirit ND divestiture risk and Trinity insourcing trend make diversification urgent.

**Key person risk is severe.** Rich Murray as 100% sole owner with all hospital relationships flowing through him represents what Scope Research characterizes as companies with "little to no enterprise value separate from the efforts of the owner/operator." Standard PE mitigations include a 15–20% equity rollover, 5-year employment and non-compete agreements, an earnout tied to contract retention and EBITDA targets, and systematic relationship-mapping to institutionalize client touchpoints. A COO/VP Operations hire within six months of close is essential.

**The 1099 contractor classification risk is material.** With 147 of 163 workers classified as independent contractors (90%), HealthSource carries significant exposure. Emergency medicine has the highest 1099 rate of any specialty (~20.5% per AMA), but the total percentage here is extreme. Under the ABC test used in states like California, New Jersey, and Massachusetts, physicians performing the company's core business likely fail the "outside usual course of business" prong. The Trump DOL issued Field Assistance Bulletin 2025-1 in May 2025 reverting to more employer-friendly classification standards, and formal rescission of the stricter Biden-era 2024 rule is pending. However, the 2024 rule remains in effect for private litigation, and state enforcement continues independently. **Reclassification would increase compensation costs by 20–35%**, destroying EBITDA margins. A pre-close classification audit is essential, and buyers should model the financial impact of full W-2 conversion. Virginia precedent includes a **$7.2 million fine** for misclassifying 1,105 nurses; a Pennsylvania case involved an alleged $19 million judgment.

**Regulatory and reimbursement risks compound.** North Dakota enforces the Corporate Practice of Medicine doctrine, requiring careful structuring of both the PRG and MSO revenue models. Expansion into new states requires state-by-state CPOM analysis (33 states have regulations, with California, Texas, and New York being particularly aggressive). The 2026 Medicare Physician Fee Schedule includes reduced facility practice expense RVUs that will cut emergency department visit payments by approximately **3%** and critical care services by **~7%**. Medicare physician payments have effectively declined over **30% since 2001** when adjusted for inflation, creating persistent margin pressure for companies serving high-Medicare-mix rural populations.

**Geographic concentration** in four North Dakota hospitals exposes the company to localized risks: rural population decline, limited physician workforce availability (only 9–10% of U.S. physicians practice rurally despite 20% of the population living in rural areas), and 12 of North Dakota's 39 rural hospitals being at risk of closure. **Growth execution risk** is also substantial—expanding from 7 to 27 clinical practices requires scaling from 16 salaried employees to a significantly larger institutional infrastructure, addressing working capital demands (weekly payroll against 60–90 day hospital payment cycles), and recruiting physicians to rural locations where 69% of hospital CEOs identify recruitment as their top challenge.

## Conclusion: a defensible niche platform requiring disciplined execution

HealthSource Partners occupies a genuinely attractive niche—rural and semi-urban healthcare staffing with contracted revenue—at a time when the competitive landscape has been cleared by the failures of overleveraged national competitors. The land-and-expand model with CommonSpirit and Trinity (or their affiliates) provides a credible growth thesis, and the structural physician shortage in rural markets creates durable demand.

However, the investment thesis hinges on several assumptions that require rigorous validation. First, the Trinity Health relationship must be clarified—if the client is the small Minot, ND system rather than the national Livonia system, the growth addressable market narrative changes fundamentally. Second, CommonSpirit's active divestiture of North Dakota hospitals creates near-term revenue continuity risk that must be assessed contract-by-contract. Third, the 90% 1099 contractor workforce represents a latent liability that could materially impair EBITDA if challenged.

A PE buyer should target **5–7x trailing EBITDA** as the entry multiple, reflecting the company's compelling growth trajectory offset by its concentrated risk profile. This implies a **$14M–$20M enterprise value on 2024 EBITDA**, scaling to $28M–$50M if the buyer is willing to underwrite 2025–2026 forward projections at 7–8x with appropriate contractual protections. The deal structure should include significant escrow for 1099 liability, an earnout tied to contract retention and client diversification milestones, mandatory founder equity rollover of 15–20%, and pre-close completion of a regulatory compliance audit, 1099 classification analysis, and FMV opinions on all hospital contracts.

The most important lesson from the PE graveyard in physician staffing is that this is a relationship business operating on thin margins. **Moderate leverage (≤4x EBITDA)**, in-network revenue positioning, physician autonomy preservation, and patient organic growth—not financial engineering—are the only strategies that have survived the post-2022 reckoning. Kinderhook Industries' Rural Healthcare Group, which recently bid $245M for Steward Medical Group out of bankruptcy, demonstrates that dedicated PE interest in rural healthcare platforms exists. HealthSource could be positioned as a similar platform—but only with disciplined capital structure, rapid customer diversification, and institutional management buildout beyond its founder.
