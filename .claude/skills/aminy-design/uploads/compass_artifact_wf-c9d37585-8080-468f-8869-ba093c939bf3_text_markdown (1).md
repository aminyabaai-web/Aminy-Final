# BCBA compensation structures in ABA therapy: a 2025–2026 market guide

Arizona-based ABA companies should target **$75,000–$100,000 base salary** for full-time BCBAs, with total compensation reaching **$90,000–$125,000** when bonuses and benefits are included — reflecting a market where demand outpaces supply by roughly 60% nationwide and the state has only **~1,600 certified BCBAs** serving an estimated 800 clients each. The most competitive employers are structuring compensation around **25 billable hours per week** for salaried in-home BCBAs and **27–30 for clinic-based**, with uncapped monthly production bonuses starting at **$25–$40 per billable hour over minimum**. Companies transitioning from legacy hourly models to salaried or hybrid structures should plan for a **6-month rollout** with a **12–18 month grandfather window** and **$2,000–$5,000 retention bonuses** to prevent attrition during the shift.

ZipRecruiter places the statewide average lower at **$83,008/year**, ranking Arizona **50th of 50 states** — a figure that likely understates Phoenix-metro reality, where job postings routinely advertise **$80,000–$130,000**. ## 2. How leading ABA companies structure salaried compensation and bonuses

The competitive landscape reveals a consistent architecture across major employers: base salary in the **$80,000–$95,000** range, performance bonuses of **$12,000–$18,000 per year**, signing bonuses of **$5,000–$10,000**, and benefits packages valued at **$10,000–$20,000** annually. Here is what specific competitors with Arizona presence are paying:

**Action Behavior Centers** offers base salary of **$84,000–$88,000** with total compensation reaching **$80,000–$117,000+**. Their standout feature is an uncapped monthly production bonus that kicks in at just **26 billable hours per week** — a deliberately low threshold that makes bonus attainment realistic. Maximum caseload is capped at **8 clients** per BCBA with an average of 5.5. BCBAs get **29 days off in Year 1** and up to **52 work-from-home days per year**. At the Senior BCBA level, employees become eligible for equity ownership.

**Centria Autism** (with a Peoria, AZ location) pays **$80,000–$95,000 base** for center-based BCBAs and **$80,000–$90,000** for in-home, with their premium early-intervention division (Life Skills Autism Academy) reaching **$90,000–$100,000**. Their bonus model averages **~$1,000/month** ($12,000/year) through a "Pay for Performance" system that credits billable-hour averages during PTO — a nuanced retention feature. Standard signing bonus is **$10,000**.

**BlueSprig / LEARN Behavioral** posts **$85,000–$90,000 base** with monthly performance bonuses up to **$16,000–$18,000/year**, yielding total compensation of **$82,000–$113,000+**. Regional Support BCBAs earn **$90,000–$95,000** base. **Trumpet Behavioral Health** (affiliated with BlueSprig, with Phoenix presence) pays **$80,000–$95,000 base** plus bonus opportunity up to **$17,000/year**, with total comp reaching **$112,000**.

**Hopebridge** (telehealth positions available in Phoenix) advertises total compensation of **$77,000–$118,750** with clinician-designed bi-weekly bonuses and an industry-leading **30 days PTO** in Year 1. **Butterfly Effects** offers the highest total comp ceiling at **$128,000** for Center Director roles ($100,000–$110,000 base plus up to $18,000 in quarterly incentives), along with unlimited PTO.

**Positive Behavior Support Corp (PBS)**, an Arizona operator, pays up to **$95,000** for BCBAs in the state, plus up to **$10,000 relocation bonus** with a 1-year commitment and an additional **$5,000** at the 1-year mark. As a BCBA-owned company (no PE investors), they differentiate by paying salary while BCBAs build their caseload.

The bonus structure taxonomy across these companies falls into four models:

- **Per-hour overage**: The most direct approach. Peak Behavioral Health offers up to **$40/hour** for each billable hour above a 25-hour weekly average. Other companies range from **$15–$40 per overage hour**.
- **Monthly/bi-weekly production bonuses**: Action Behavior Centers, Centria, and BlueSprig use this model, paying **$750–$1,500/month** for meeting productivity targets.
- **Quarterly incentive pools**: Butterfly Effects and others pay **$4,000–$18,000 annually** in quarterly installments tied to utilization, client outcomes, and staff retention.
- **Tiered level systems**: Apollo Behavior structures target earnings by clinical level — CS1: $94,000; CS2: $98,000; CS3: $102,000; Sr. CS-2: **$115,000** — giving BCBAs a transparent path from hire to senior clinician.

## 3. Billable hour requirements and the non-billable time budget

The industry standard for **full-time salaried BCBAs** is **25 billable hours per week**, with in-home roles typically targeting **23–27 hours** and clinic-based roles targeting **27–30 hours**. A survey by ABA Resource Center found that **66% of BCBAs** prefer positions requiring **23–28 billable hours per week**, making this the competitive sweet spot for recruitment.

For a salaried in-home BCBA working 40 total hours per week with a 25-billable-hour target, the non-billable time budget is approximately **15 hours per week (37.5%)**, allocated roughly as follows: **3–5 hours** for documentation and treatment planning, **3–4 hours** for drive time between clients, **2–3 hours** for supervision of RBTs, **2–3 hours** for parent training prep and team meetings, and **1–2 hours** for administrative tasks. Clinic-based BCBAs typically have **10–13 non-billable hours** (25–32% of total), as drive time is eliminated.

The billable-to-total-hours ratio is a critical metric: **63–71%** for in-home BCBAs and **68–75%** for clinic-based. ABA practice management consultant Hi-5 ABA flags that non-billable time compensation exceeding **15% of total payroll** begins eroding margins, suggesting companies should target the lower end of non-billable percentages through efficient scheduling and documentation systems.

In-home BCBAs carry inherently higher overhead. The companies most successfully managing this differential either **pay a modest premium** for in-home work ($2,000–$5,000 above clinic base), **reduce billable hour requirements** by 2–4 hours per week relative to clinic staff, or **bundle mileage reimbursement and drive-time pay** as additional compensation that effectively narrows the in-home/clinic gap.

## 4. Part-time hourly BCBA models and how to handle non-billable time

Part-time hourly BCBA rates in Arizona range from **$80–$110/hour** for billable time in contractor/1099 arrangements, and **$50–$85/hour** for W-2 employees. The significant gap reflects the fact that 1099 rates must offset the lack of benefits, employer taxes, and non-billable pay. Nationally, W-2 hourly BCBAs average **$42.82/hour** (ZipRecruiter) when including all paid hours.

Five distinct models exist for handling non-billable time for hourly BCBAs:

**The split-rate model** is the most common for W-2 hourly employees. Companies pay a higher rate for billable hours and a lower rate for non-billable work. A typical structure: **$65–$80/hour billable** and **$25–$35/hour non-billable**. FLSA requires that all hours worked by W-2 employees be compensated, but permits different rates as long as non-billable pay meets or exceeds minimum wage. Example: A BCBA billing 25 hours at $75 and doing 10 non-billable hours at $30 earns **$2,175/week** ($113,100 annualized).

**The bundled-rate (salaried) model** wraps everything into one salary. A BCBA earning $85,000 with benefits valued at $12,000–$15,000 has effective total compensation of **$97,000–$100,000**. The advantage is simplicity and income predictability; the disadvantage is that high-producing BCBAs may feel underpaid relative to peers on hourly models.

**The billable-only model** (1099 contractors) pays premium rates of **$80–$110/hour** for billable time only. ABA Resource Center explicitly warns that applying this model to W-2 employees — paying only for billable time while expecting unpaid admin work — **violates FLSA**. Yet some companies persist in this practice, creating legal exposure.

**Flat admin stipends** provide a fixed weekly or monthly payment for non-billable work. While less common, this model offers predictability — for example, a **$200–$400/week admin stipend** on top of billable hourly pay. This simplifies payroll while acknowledging that non-billable work is real work.

**Ratio-based models** set an expected billable-to-admin ratio — typically **3:1 or 4:1** — meaning for every 3–4 billable hours, 1 hour of admin is paid. This model mathematically resembles the split-rate approach but frames expectations differently for the employee.

For **mileage reimbursement**, the IRS standard rate is **$0.70/mile for 2025** and **$0.725/mile for 2026**. Most in-home ABA companies reimburse at the IRS rate for travel between clients, though the first and last commute of the day is typically excluded. Drive time between clients **must be compensated as work hours** for W-2 employees under FLSA — usually at the non-billable rate.

**Best practice minimum hour requirements for PT BCBAs** range from **10–15 billable hours per week**. Companies typically require a **minimum weekly commitment** to justify the administrative overhead of maintaining the employee's caseload, credentialing, and supervision structure.

## 5. Transitioning from legacy hourly to salaried or hybrid models

Mid-size ABA companies with approximately 50 staff face a specific challenge: their BCBAs may have been hired at varying hourly rates over several years, creating internal pay equity issues that a structured transition must resolve. The recommended approach is a **6-month phased transition** with a **dual-track endpoint** — salaried full-time and hourly part-time — that gives every BCBA a viable path forward.

**The recommended timeline** proceeds in four phases. **Phase 1 (Months 1–2)** involves auditing every BCBA's actual earnings over the prior 6–12 months, including overtime, to establish true current compensation. The single most common mistake in hourly-to-salary conversions is setting salary levels that inadvertently cut total pay. Simultaneously, benchmark against the market data above and design salary bands by experience tier. **Phase 2 (Months 2–3)** focuses on designing the new structure: salary tiers, billable hour expectations, bonus triggers, benefits packages, and the PT hourly alternative track. Have employment counsel review FLSA classification. **Phase 3 (Months 3–5)** is the communication rollout — an **11-week structured communication plan** beginning with leadership briefings, followed by a company-wide announcement, town halls, and crucially, **individual 1-on-1 meetings** with each BCBA showing a personalized compensation comparison (old vs. new total comp). **Phase 4 (Months 5–6)** is implementation, with the first 90 days under active monitoring.

**Grandfathering existing staff** is recommended with a **12–18 month sunset window**. During this period, existing hourly BCBAs may maintain their current pay structure while all new hires enter the new model. An **early-conversion bonus of $2,000–$5,000** incentivizes voluntary adoption. At the sunset date, BCBAs must choose the salaried track or the PT hourly track. The grandfather period reduces flight risk but increases administrative complexity — running dual payroll models — so keep it time-bounded.

**For BCBAs who don't want full-time**, the PT hourly track should be genuinely attractive, not punitive. Set the hourly rate at **$55–$85/hour** (higher per-hour than the salaried equivalent to offset the lack of benefits), with flexible scheduling of **10–25 hours/week** and a split-rate or stipend model for non-billable time. Include PT BCBAs in team meetings, CEU opportunities, and culture events. Research consistently shows that **67% of BCBAs who leave cite reasons other than compensation** — autonomy, work-life balance, and professional development matter equally.

**Retention strategies that work during transitions** include stay bonuses of **$2,000–$5,000** vesting at 6-month and 12-month milestones, structured as "golden handcuffs" requiring repayment if the BCBA leaves before the vesting date. **Student loan repayment assistance** is an increasingly powerful differentiator given that BCBAs carry graduate-level debt. Clear career pathways with defined levels — BCBA → Senior BCBA → Lead BCBA → Clinical Director — can reduce turnover by up to **30%** when coupled with transparent criteria for advancement. One ABA company reported losing **10 employees overnight** after botching a compensation rollout with inadequate communication, underscoring that process matters as much as the numbers.

## 6. FLSA classification and Arizona-specific legal considerations

BCBAs almost certainly qualify for the **Learned Professional Exemption** under FLSA, which requires meeting three tests: salary basis (paid a fixed salary, not hourly), salary level (at least **$684/week or $35,568/year** — the threshold after federal courts struck down the DOL's proposed increase to $43,888), and a duties test requiring advanced knowledge in a field of science acquired through prolonged specialized instruction. BCBAs hold **master's degrees or higher**, exercise independent clinical judgment, develop individualized treatment plans, and supervise other professionals — squarely meeting the duties test. However, the critical prerequisite is that they **must actually be paid on a salary basis**. A BCBA paid hourly at any rate is generally not exempt, regardless of duties. Converting from hourly to salary is what enables the exempt classification.

Arizona follows **federal FLSA** for overtime with no additional state-level overtime statute. The state's **minimum wage is $14.70/hour** as of January 2025. Arizona is an **at-will employment state**, permitting compensation structure changes with adequate notice and documentation. Under Arizona's Fair Wages and Healthy Families Act, all employees — including salaried — accrue **paid sick leave**. Final paycheck requirements mandate settlement of outstanding wages within **7 working days** of termination or by the end of the next pay period.

## 7. The supply-demand squeeze reshaping Arizona BCBA compensation

The macro picture is unambiguous: the ABA industry is in a structural labor shortage that will persist for years. The CDC now reports **1 in 31 children** identified with ASD (up from 1 in 36 in 2020 and 1 in 150 in 2000), driving exponential growth in demand for ABA services. BCBA job postings have grown at a **compounded annual rate of 44.2% since 2017**, and the BACB's February 2026 report documents **132,307 unduplicated job postings** in 2025 against **81,566 total certificants** — a demand-supply gap of approximately 60%. Arizona's **~1,600 BCBAs** serving the state's autism population yield a ratio of roughly **800 clients per BCBA**, one of the most strained in the country.

Private equity has dramatically reshaped the competitive landscape. PE firms drove **85% of ABA M&A deals from 2017–2022**, pushing the market toward consolidation and standardized comp structures. PE-backed companies compete aggressively for BCBAs with inflated salaries and signing bonuses, creating upward pressure that collides with **stagnant insurance reimbursement rates** (national Medicaid average: ~$63/hour for direct therapy). ABA centers operate on **~15% profit margins**, meaning compensation decisions directly impact viability. Mid-size companies like one with 50 staff must compete on total value proposition — culture, flexibility, career growth, manageable caseloads — not just salary, because they cannot outbid PE-backed consolidators on base pay alone.

The replacement cost for losing a BCBA ranges from **30–200% of annual salary** ($22,500–$200,000+), and CentralReach's 2025 market report shows mid-size ABA organizations averaging **89.3% annual turnover**. Nearly **50% of BCBAs leave within their first two years**. These economics make a well-designed compensation structure not merely an HR exercise but a survival imperative.

## Conclusion: key design principles for an Arizona compensation restructure

The data points to several clear conclusions. First, an Arizona-based company should set salary bands at **$70,000–$80,000** for entry-level BCBAs, **$80,000–$95,000** for mid-career, and **$95,000–$120,000** for senior/director roles to remain competitive — calibrated upward from national medians to reflect Phoenix-area cost of living and the state's acute BCBA shortage. Second, billable hour expectations should target **25 hours/week for in-home** and **28 hours/week for clinic** — below the 30-hour threshold that drives dissatisfaction — with production bonuses of **$25–$40 per overage hour** to reward high performers without punishing average ones. Third, the split-rate model (e.g., **$70/hr billable, $30/hr non-billable**) is the most transparent and FLSA-compliant approach for PT hourly BCBAs, with mileage at the IRS rate and drive time paid at the non-billable rate. Fourth, a dual-track structure with **genuine parity of respect** between salaried and PT tracks prevents the hemorrhaging of BCBAs who value flexibility. And fifth, the transition itself requires as much strategic attention as the final structure — a **6-month phased rollout**, personalized compensation comparisons, retention bonuses, and a **12–18 month grandfather window** are table stakes for maintaining stability through the change.
