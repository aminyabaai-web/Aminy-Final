# Restructuring BCBA compensation: a complete operational playbook

**For an Arizona ABA company restructuring compensation, the core challenge is balancing aggressive productivity standards with clinical quality, retention, and market realities.** The data paints a clear picture: a single BCBA enables **$750K–$1M+ in annual revenue** through supervised RBTs, the cost of losing one runs **$65K–$90K per month** in lost revenue, and Arizona faces the **2nd-highest projected BCBA demand growth** in the nation at 39.2% through 2032. Getting compensation right isn't just an HR exercise — it's the single biggest lever for financial viability. Below is a comprehensive, data-driven breakdown across all five areas of inquiry.

---

## 1. Sign-on bonuses range from $3K to $25K with standardized vesting structures

The ABA industry has settled into fairly predictable sign-on bonus tiers. **The most common full-time BCBA sign-on bonus is $5,000–$10,000**, with outliers reaching $25,000 in extreme shortage markets. Arizona-specific postings cluster in the $5K–$10K range for full-time roles.

### Vesting structures fall into four models

The **90-day lump sum** is most common — the full bonus pays after 90 days of employment, subject to a 12-month clawback if the BCBA leaves. The **quarterly installment model** splits the bonus into $1,000 increments over 3–4 quarters (used by New Horizons ABA). A **6-month full-payment** model is also used, particularly in Arizona. The most innovative approach comes from Fiesta Health, which offers a **"Love It or Leave It" guarantee** — the BCBA keeps the bonus even if they leave after 90 days, removing risk from the employee's perspective entirely.

**Clawback provisions** are nearly universal. The standard is a **12-month full-repayment requirement** if the BCBA voluntarily resigns or is terminated for cause. More sophisticated programs use **tapering clawbacks**: 100% repayment in months 1–6, 50% in months 7–12. Arizona does not prohibit clawback provisions but requires them in a written agreement signed at hire.

### Part-time BCBAs do receive sign-on bonuses — but rarely

Several companies offer PT sign-on bonuses. My Team ABA offers a **$1,000 flat bonus** for both FT and PT BCBAs. Spirit ABA Therapy (Arizona) advertises sign-on bonuses for part-time roles. Beth Israel Lahey Health explicitly states its bonus is **"pro-rated for part time."** The standard pro-rating method is straightforward: a PT BCBA at 0.5 FTE receives **50% of the FT bonus amount**. Some companies use a fixed lower amount ($1,000–$2,500 for PT vs. $5,000–$10,000 for FT) rather than a formula.

### Tying bonuses to billable hour minimums is emerging but not yet standard

Most companies tie sign-on bonuses to **tenure milestones** (90 days, 6 months) rather than billable output. However, Centria's requirement of 25 billable hours within 40 days effectively makes productivity a prerequisite for continued employment and thus for retaining the bonus. **The industry trend is to use separate ongoing performance bonuses** — not sign-on bonuses — as the vehicle for billable hour incentives. Action Behavior Centers' monthly performance bonus kicks in at 26 billable hours/week; PBS Corp's quarterly bonus requires 25 hours/week at 100%.

### Referral bonuses average $1,000–$2,500 for BCBAs

The most detailed referral program found is Achieve Beyond's: **$1,000 for a BCBA referral**, paid only after the referred BCBA maintains **3+ cases for 3 months** — tying payment to production, not just retention. For RBT referrals, $200–$500 is standard. Unlike sign-on bonuses, referral bonuses typically have **no clawback provisions** — the bonus simply doesn't pay if the referred employee leaves before the vesting milestone.

## 2. A single BCBA enables $750K–$1M+ in annual revenue through RBT supervision

This is the most important economic insight for compensation design. **A BCBA's direct billing represents only 15–20% of their total economic value.** The other 80–85% comes from the RBT services they make possible through supervision.

### BACB supervision requirements create the economic multiplier

The BACB requires that **5% of each RBT's total monthly service hours** be supervised by a BCBA. An RBT billing 25 hours/week (108 hours/month) requires approximately **5.4 hours of supervision per month**. Supervision must include at least **2 face-to-face contacts per month**, with at least 1 direct observation of the RBT working with a client. Group supervision (up to 10 RBTs) may constitute no more than 50% of total supervision hours.

The practical industry ratio is **6–8 RBTs per full-time BCBA**. CASP recommends 6–12 clients for comprehensive ABA and 10–15 for focused treatment. At 7 RBTs each billing 25 hours/week, the BCBA's supervision load is approximately **9.5 hours/week** — leaving room for their own 25 billable hours within a 40-hour week, but just barely.

### Arizona billing rates by CPT code

AHCCCS (Arizona Medicaid) rates, converted to hourly, show significant variation by modifier:

| CPT Code | Service | Low Rate (HM) | Mid Rate (HN) | High Rate (HO/BCBA) |
|----------|---------|---------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **97153** | RBT direct treatment | $71.64/hr | $85.28/hr | $94.76/hr |
| **97155** | BCBA supervision | $100.20/hr | $119.28/hr | $149.12/hr |
| **97151** | BCBA assessment | $120.24/hr | $143.12/hr | $178.92/hr |
| **97156** | Parent training | — | — | ~$100.56/hr |

Facility-based rates run approximately **11–15% higher** (e.g., 97153 at facility: up to $105.72/hr). Private insurance typically pays **20–40% above Medicaid**, putting estimated private rates at $85–$110/hr for RBT services and $130–$170/hr for BCBA services.

### The revenue math makes the case for retention investment

Using conservative AHCCCS rates with the HO modifier:

| Revenue Source | Weekly | Annual (50 weeks) |
|---------------|--------|-------------------|
| 7 RBTs × 25 hrs × $94.76/hr | $16,583 | **$829,150** |
| BCBA own billing: 25 hrs × $149.12/hr | $3,728 | **$186,400** |
| **Total revenue enabled by one BCBA** | **$20,311** | **$1,015,550** |

At a blended rate including private insurance, total revenue per BCBA easily exceeds **$1 million annually**. The BCBA's salary ($83K–$94K in Arizona) represents roughly **8–12% of the revenue they enable** — a **supervision multiplier of 5–7×** their own direct billing.

### When a BCBA leaves, the financial damage is immediate and severe

All supervised RBT services are at risk without BCBA oversight. **Monthly revenue at risk: $65,000–$90,000.** Replacement timelines run **4–9 months** when factoring recruiting (2–4 months), payer credentialing (1–3 months), and caseload ramp-up (1–2 months). Total replacement cost ranges from **$25,000 to $188,000** per BCBA, including lost revenue, recruiting costs, and productivity ramp. With approximately **74,000 certified BCBAs** nationally against **103,000+ job openings**, the market gives departing BCBAs enormous leverage.

## 3. Non-billable time is not discretionary — accreditors and ethics codes mandate it

The question isn't whether to allow non-billable time but how much is genuinely required versus inflated. **Industry benchmarks place sustainable BCBA utilization at 62–75%**, meaning **25–38% of a BCBA's time is non-billable by necessity.**

### What BHCOE and CASP actually require

BHCOE's 201 Standards of Excellence (effective April 2025, now under CASP ownership) create non-billable obligations across multiple domains. The "Must Pass" standards include: processes for data collection and analysis procedures (F.06), caseloads that enable appropriate supervision (F.10), standard documentation templates for assessments and progress reports (G.01–G.05), and regular employee satisfaction measurement (D.10). CASP's ABA Practice Guidelines v3.0 (April 2024) add requirements for comprehensive assessment before treatment, individualized treatment plans with measurable goals, ongoing transition/discharge planning, and coordination with other healthcare providers.

### The BACB Ethics Code creates substantial non-billable obligations

The 2022 Ethics Code contains **85 standards across 6 sections**. Key provisions generating non-billable time include: maintaining competence through professional development (1.06), cultural responsiveness training (1.07), graphing and analyzing data for clinical decisions (2.17), continual evaluation of interventions (2.18), detailed documentation of all professional activities (3.11), and comprehensive supervision documentation retained for **7 years** (4.05). Section 3.03 explicitly limits caseload acceptance to "available resources including time for case supervision," directly capping billable hours.

### Required versus discretionary non-billable activities

**Mandatory non-billable activities** (regulatory, ethical, or payer-required) include: treatment plan development and updates (every 6 months for reauthorization), reassessments with standardized measures (VB-MAPP, Vineland — every 3–6 months), progress reports for insurance reauthorization, RBT supervision preparation and documentation, data analysis and graphing (~30 minutes/week per client), parent training preparation, insurance authorization submissions, and 32 CEUs per 2-year certification cycle.

**Strongly recommended** activities (for accreditation and best practice) include team meetings, collaboration with other providers (OT, SLP, school), quality assurance reviews, and discharge/transition planning. **Truly discretionary** activities — internal newsletters, social media, research projects, committee participation — can be eliminated without clinical or regulatory consequence.

Per-client non-billable time over a 6-month authorization cycle totals approximately **40–64 hours**, or roughly **1.5–2.5 hours per client per week**. For a BCBA with 8–10 clients, this alone accounts for **12–25 hours per week** of non-billable clinical work.

### Sustainable billable targets by setting

| Setting | Billable Hours/Week | Non-Billable | Total | Utilization |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|-------|-------------|
| In-home/community | 23–27 | 13–17 | 40 | 58–68% |
| Clinic-based | 27–30 | 10–13 | 40 | 68–75% |
| Low-burnout model | 20–22 | 18–20 | 40 | 50–55% |

**Each 1-hour reduction in weekly billable expectations costs approximately $6,000/year per BCBA** in lost revenue (at ~$120/hr blended rate). For a team of 10 BCBAs, that's $60,000/year per hour — making the calibration of billable targets a high-stakes decision.

### Handling the "you don't care about quality" pushback

The most effective reframe: **billable hours ARE quality care**. If a child is authorized for 25 hours/week and receives only 18, the quality failure is underdelivery, not overwork. The BACB Ethics Code (Section 2.02) explicitly requires timely service delivery. Companies should pair productivity requirements with a **Clinical Quality Dashboard** tracking treatment plan timeliness, parent training completion, client outcome data, and supervision quality scores — proving that productivity and quality are measured together, not in opposition. Structured non-billable time blocks (e.g., Friday mornings for reports) and administrative support (scheduling, insurance verification) also reduce the perception of being squeezed.

## 4. Part-time BCBAs become profitable at 0.6 FTE — below that, use contractors

The economics of part-time BCBAs are driven by **fixed overhead costs that don't scale with hours worked**. Understanding the break-even point is critical for setting minimum FTE requirements.

### Fixed overhead per BCBA runs $8,000–$18,000 annually

| Cost Category | Annual Cost | Notes |
|--------------|-------------|-------|
| Insurance credentialing | $500–$2,500/payer (one-time) | 90–150 day timeline; Medicaid up to 180 days |
| Lost revenue during credentialing | $15K–$20K/month | Major hidden cost; 3–6 months of zero billing |
| Professional liability insurance | $100–$500/year | Per BCBA |
| IT setup + software | $1,500–$3,000 one-time + $240–$576/year | Laptop, practice management software |
| HR/admin/payroll overhead | $3,000–$8,000/year | Recruiting, onboarding, management time |
| CEU/license renewal (if company-paid) | $500–$2,000/year | Retention tool |

The **credentialing delay is the largest hidden cost**. Each month a BCBA is uncredentialed represents $15K–$20K in unbillable revenue. For a PT BCBA working limited hours, this investment takes proportionally longer to recoup.

### Break-even and profitability thresholds

At a blended net effective rate of ~$75/hr after billing fees and denials, a BCBA needs only **~14 billable hours per month** to cover their fixed overhead — trivially low. But the real question is profitability after labor costs. At Arizona PT BCBA rates of $50–$110/hr:

- **0.5 FTE (20 hrs/week, ~15 billable)**: Revenue ~$62K/year. After PT pay ($45K–$55K) and overhead ($12K), contribution is **breakeven to slightly negative**.
- **0.6 FTE (24 hrs/week, ~18–20 billable)**: Revenue ~$83K/year. After PT pay ($55K–$66K) and overhead ($12K), contribution is **$5K–$15K positive**.
- **0.75 FTE (30 hrs/week, ~22–25 billable)**: Clearly profitable, approaching FT economics.

**Recommended policy: Set 0.5 FTE (20 hours/week) as the minimum for W-2 employment, with 0.6 FTE preferred.** Below 0.5 FTE, use 1099 contractor arrangements to avoid carrying fixed overhead for marginal contributors. Credential PT BCBAs with only the top 3–4 payers rather than all panels.

### Innovative staffing models worth considering

**Telehealth supervision** eliminates drive time and allows PT BCBAs to cover wider geography. Arizona permits telehealth ABA supervision, and CMS has made telehealth permanent for ABA codes. A PT BCBA doing 100% telehealth supervision can be **30–40% more productive per hour** than one driving between homes. **Supervision-only roles** (no direct caseload, purely overseeing RBTs) maximize the BCBA multiplier effect. **Contract-to-hire** — starting BCBAs as contractors during the 90–150 day credentialing period, then converting to W-2 once billing begins — reduces financial risk on new hires.

### PE-backed firms versus independent practices differ sharply on flexibility

PE-backed companies typically enforce **28–32 billable hours/week**, are less tolerant of PT arrangements, and push standardized treatment plans for efficiency. Some have failed spectacularly — CARD went bankrupt after Blackstone's acquisition loaded $160M in debt and aggressive cost-cutting gutted clinical quality. Independent practices offer more flexibility but struggle with the administrative overhead of managing PT staff. **The sweet spot**: use PE-level operational infrastructure (technology, credentialing systems, scheduling optimization) but with independent-practice-level clinical autonomy and scheduling flexibility.

### Arizona's BCBA market demands flexibility

Arizona ranks **50th out of 50 states for BCBA salaries** (ZipRecruiter) while projecting **39.2% job growth** through 2032. This supply-demand mismatch means Arizona companies must compete on flexibility, not just pay. Top-paying Arizona cities include Glendale, Chandler, and Scottsdale (9–11% above state average). The FT salary range in Phoenix is $77K–$124K (Glassdoor), while PT rates run $50–$110/hr. **Key differentiators for attracting BCBAs in Arizona**: manageable caseloads (6–10 clients), clinical autonomy, CEU reimbursement, and flexible scheduling.

## 5. Managing legacy staff through turnaround requires a phased 6-month approach

ABA already has **65% median annual turnover** (BHCOE 2022). During a compensation restructuring, expect an **additional 15–25% voluntary turnover** concentrated in the first 60–90 days after announcement. The goal isn't zero turnover — it's ensuring the right people stay.

### The 90-day phase-up model for existing staff

Rather than implementing new standards overnight, use a graduated approach while holding all new hires to the full standard from day one:

- **Month 1**: Legacy staff at **75% of new billable target** (e.g., ~19 hrs if target is 25). Focus on training, scheduling optimization, and administrative support deployment.
- **Month 2**: Move to **85% of target** (~21 hrs). Bi-weekly check-ins identify who's on track.
- **Month 3**: Move to **95% of target** (~24 hrs). Those meeting this threshold are fully transitioned.
- **Month 4**: Full standard applies to everyone equally. 60–90 day PIPs for those consistently below target.
- **Months 5–6**: PIPs conclude. Separate from non-performers.

**Two-tier standards are legally permissible in Arizona** (an at-will state) as long as the tiers are based on hire date — a neutral criterion — not protected characteristics. Document the business rationale and have an employment attorney draft written acknowledgments.

### Retention tools that prevent losing your best people

**Retention bonuses** are the most effective tool. For a BCBA earning $80K, offer **$8,000–$15,000** paid in three installments: $3K–$5K at 90 days into the new structure, $3K–$5K at 6 months, and $2K–$5K at 12 months, with clawback for voluntary resignation before each vesting date. **Guaranteed minimum compensation** during the first 90 days — holding BCBAs harmless at their current pay regardless of whether they hit new targets — removes fear and creates psychological safety for the transition.

**Identify and protect your top 20% immediately.** These BCBAs get higher retention bonuses (20–25% of salary), leadership roles in the transition, first pick of caseloads, title upgrades, and personal outreach from their direct supervisor within the first week of announcing changes. The voluntary turnover during restructuring will be disproportionately lower performers — which is actually desirable — but only if top performers feel secure.

### Communication strategy is everything

**Never announce compensation changes via email.** Hold small-group meetings (10–15 people maximum) led by the **Clinical Director, not the CFO** — the messenger determines whether the change is perceived as clinical improvement or corporate cost-cutting. Lead with "why": frame the restructuring around authorization utilization (families approved for 25 hours/week should receive 25 hours), client outcomes, and long-term company sustainability. **Show each BCBA personalized modeling** of what their compensation looks like under the new structure at various productivity levels — the model must demonstrate that a productive BCBA can earn equal to or more than before.

Form a **Clinical Standards Committee** of 3–5 respected BCBAs before finalizing standards. Give them genuine input on non-billable time allocation, caseload structure, and administrative burden reduction. Their endorsement carries more weight than any management directive. Announce on Monday or Tuesday (never Friday), provide 30 days notice before new compensation takes effect, and run Q&A sessions.

### Legal guardrails for Arizona

Arizona requires no specific advance notice period for compensation changes, but **best practice is 30 days written notice** (60 days for changes exceeding 10% potential reduction). All changes must be prospective — retroactive pay reduction for hours already worked is illegal. Have each BCBA sign a written acknowledgment of the new structure including effective date, details, expectations, and transition timeline. No severance is legally required, but offering it to those who choose to leave reduces litigation risk.

The BACB Ethics Code actually **supports** reasonable productivity expectations. Section 2.02 requires timely service delivery; Section 1.04 requires operating within employer-defined roles. Critically, Section 2.14 states that BCBAs who believe organizational requirements create ethical conflicts must raise concerns through proper channels — not simply refuse compliance. Set billable targets within the **25–30 hour/week industry standard**, include adequate non-billable allocation, and create an Ethics Review Process where BCBAs can flag cases where productivity pressures may genuinely conflict with client needs.

## Conclusion: the numbers that matter most

The financial case for getting BCBA compensation right is overwhelming. Each BCBA enables **$750K–$1M+ in annual revenue** while costing $83K–$94K in salary — a ratio that justifies significant investment in retention. The **$65K–$90K monthly revenue loss** from a BCBA vacancy, combined with a **4–9 month replacement timeline**, means that a $15,000 retention bonus during a restructuring pays for itself in under one week of prevented vacancy.

For part-time BCBAs, **0.6 FTE is the minimum profitable threshold** for W-2 employment — below that, use contractor arrangements. Sign-on bonuses of **$5,000–$10,000** with 12-month clawbacks are market-competitive for Arizona. Non-billable time at **25–38% of total hours** is not discretionary padding — it's the cost of BACB compliance, accreditation maintenance, and clinical quality that keeps authorizations flowing.

The turnaround playbook has a clear sequence: identify and protect top performers first, form a clinical advisory committee second, announce with 30 days notice third, implement via 90-day phase-up fourth, and separate from non-performers by month six. **The BCBAs you lose during restructuring will disproportionately be those who weren't meeting standards anyway — as long as you've given your best people reasons to stay.**
